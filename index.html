Redesigning MOBA

The Museum of Bad Art (MOBA) is a non-profit devoted to the upliftment of art that "would not be displayed and appreciated in any other forum". In this redesign, I address the *Become a Friend* page, which allows you to manage your newsletter subscription status and donate to MOBA. Unfortunately, for such an important page, it features a confusing layout and poor contrast that makes it difficult to process the information onscreen. Thus, with usability metrics in-hand, I set out to create a new and improved version.

Disclaimer

Critics may view this redesign as hypercritical - i.e. what's the point in redesigning a website for bad art? What if a shabby design is exactly what they're looking for - a form of bad art in itself? To that I respond: I am no art critic, but any website, no matter how artsy, can be assessed in terms of usability. To enjoy and explore their exhibitions, users must be able to glean useful information from their website. For that end, good design is indispensable/irreplaceable.

Problems with Current Interface

The current webpage features a host of usability issues, which may be broken down into the following categories.

Learnability

  • No clear text hierarchy through colors or size. Titles are all similar sizes, some are colored light green for no apparent reason.
  • Hard to navigate and gain an overview of the page - too much text on screen
  • The form to subscribe to MOBA news is duplicated in the main page and the footer, leaving users to wonder what the difference between them is

    • Memorability

  • Text, and especially the headings, have poor contrast, making them hard to read and easy to overlook. Same thing goes for the contact emails listed on the page.
  • Text hierarchy is unstructured and confusing. Some headings colored green, others black Hard to know where to look.
  • Text spans the full screen, making it hard to focus on for larger screen sizes.

    • Efficiency

  • User has to scroll and search to find the find the small donate bottom at the bottom of the page.
  • The mobile version is cut in half (leaving half the available space blank and causing text to bleed out of containers), making it uncomfortable and inefficient to read
